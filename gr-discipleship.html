Who was John the Baptist? The Archetype Christian Matthew 3:1-5 tells us, “In those days John the Baptist came, preaching in the wilderness (or desert?) of Judea… John’s clothes were made of camel’s hair, and he had a leather belt around his waist. His food was locusts and wild honey. People went out to him from Jerusalem and all Judea and the whole region of the Jordan.” Mark 1:4-7 gives us the same opening description of the prophet stating, “John the Baptist appeared in the wilderness…The whole Judean countryside and all the people of Jerusalem went out to him…John wore clothing made of camel’s hair, with a leather belt around his waist, and he ate locusts and wild honey… And this was his message: “After me comes the one more powerful than I, the straps of whose sandals I am not worthy to stoop down and untie.”” Several interesting points to note here: • Historical: There was great social unrest at the time of John the Baptists’ ministry. Roman military occupation, Herod’s extravagance and a change of governor in the region to Pilate in A.D. 26. • Location: The word translation of eremos. Even though it says “wilderness” in most translations, the traditional location of Jesus’ baptism was in Bethany across the Jordan (see Jn 1:28). The Desert of Judea/Wilderness of Judea was an area that stretched around 20 miles from Jerusalem-Bethlaham plateau down to the Jordan River the Dead Sea. The people of Qumran lived in this area too. John also baptized at “Aenon near Salim” (Jn 3:23). • Qumran Community: Often associated with the dead sea scrolls, the religious sect (Esssenes) from Qumran professed similar doctrines of repentance and baptism. So it makes sense that John could have come from this “desert” region of the Jordan valley leading to the Dead Sea area. • Essenes: Referred to by Josephus, the Essenes were a small, separatist group that grew out of the conflicts of the Maccabean revolution. They stressed strict legal observance, buty they considered the temple priesthood corrupt and rejected much of the temple ritual and sacrificial system. Their beliefs were unorthodox (belief in a war between Sons of Light and Sons of Darkness, Teacher of Rightousness, multiple Messiah’s, etc.). John’s own viewpoint of himself was such that he thought himself unworthy of even knowing Jesus and touching his feet. John 1:6 states, “There was a man sent from God whose name was John.” Matthew 3:3, Mark 1:2-3, and Luke 3:4-6 state that John was the one “who was spoken of through the prophet Isaiah: “A voice of one calling in the wilderness, ‘Prepare the way for the Lord, make straight paths for him.’” (Luke adds several other verses). John the Baptist was the first disciple of the ministry of Jesus Christ and is a good starting point for describing one portrait of a man who dedicated his life to both faith and works, in the pursuit of God’s kingdom. John (the Apostle) tells us that John (the Baptist) was “sent from God.” Matthew describes John as a preacher who lived in the woods whom the people around the Jordan went out to see and hear preach. The latter must have been written to demonstrate that John was out there preaching the truth of the Gospel (even before the Gospel was brought into the world!). Both Matthew and Mark tell us about John’s appearance and how he most likely was relying solely on God for his provisions. We can say that John felt a call on his life to do something for the glory of God. Luke our historian, tells us, “In the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar—when Pontius Pilate was governor of Judea, Herod tetrarch of Galilee, his brother Philip tetrarch of Iturea and Traconitis, and Lysanias tetrarch of Abilene— during the high-priesthood of Annas and Caiaphas, the word of God came to John son of Zechariah in the wilderness.” Luke 3:1-2. Here, we have an interesting phrase: “the word of God came to John…in the wilderness.” John first went into the wilderness to wait on the God of the Universe to call upon him for his purpose and in so doing was able to fulfill the purpose for which he had been created. Notice how the Bible does not tell us that the word of God came to John and then we began preaching in the wilderness. John was already in the wilderness when the word of God came to him. John was likely following his heart to serve God, making his heart ready to receive God’s spirit, not knowing when that time would come or what it would look like. Since the time of Plato and Aristotle, scientists have attempted to investigate the nature of reality and human behavior and explain a priori causation of the observed nature of both phenomena. In the Posterior Analytics, Aristotle places the following crucial condition on proper knowledge: we think we have knowledge of a thing only when we have grasped its cause (APost. 71 b 9–11. Cf. APost. 94 a 20). Aristotle then expands on his investigation on causation by relying on the principle of the four causes: (1) the material cause (“that out of which…”); (2) the formal cause (“the form”, “the account of what-it-is-to-be”); (3) the efficient cause (“the primary source of the change or rest”); and (4) the final cause (“the end, that for the sake of which a thing is done”). These four causes were used by Aristotle to investigate the nature of reality that was observed. For example, “If we ask what caused a house to exist, Aristotle would give you the following answers: • Material Cause: It is made of bricks and concrete. • Formal Cause: The bricks and concrete have been assembled so that a structure has emerged. • Efficient Cause: A builder put all its parts together. • Final Cause: Its function is to be a place where we can sleep and be warm.” It is obvious that in order to explain human actions and behavior from a causality perspective, Aristotle provides no relevance to the inquiry because it fails to explain, as in the example above, why the builder decided to build the house in the first place (other than as a function of comfort). Was the builder building because he was paid to build the house, or, maybe because he lost his previous home in a flood or fire that caused him to want to build a new house in order to rest and be warm? Maybe the builder was motivated by altruistic thoughts that led him to build a house for the poor? Repentance is more than a confession of sins. It is a “turning away” from the things that offend God. It is a commitment to following the ways of Jesus Christ who lived a life as an example to those who God calls as His own. When we confess our sins we are merely acknowledging our sinful nature and our need for a savior in Jesus Christ. But, when we repent a true repentance in our hearts, we are acting out that confession in our behavior such that we are being a light in the dark world to demonstrate (fruit) of our salvation in Jesus Christ. It is not a call to be perfect and the cycle of confession and repentance will continue throughout one’s life. In order to have repentant fruit in our lives we must do two things: (1) trust in God that His ways are better than our ways and (2) trust that the Holy Spirit is leading us toward that way in which God is calling us.
